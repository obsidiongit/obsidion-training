## Who We Are

Obsidion exists because most small and mid-size businesses in specialized industries are underserved by the options available to them. Generic agencies are slow, expensive, and don't understand the constraints of a regulated market. DIY tools are cheap but leave the owner doing the work themselves with no system behind it. Vertical-specific platforms do one thing and lock you into their ecosystem. None of them solve the whole problem.

Obsidion builds the full digital infrastructure stack — custom website, local SEO, automated remarketing, and branded mobile app — as a single integrated system, purpose-built for the industries we serve. One team, one system, one outcome. The goal is to give businesses that operate in competitive and often complex markets the same caliber of digital infrastructure that larger companies take for granted, without the agency overhead, the vendor fragmentation, or the six-week timeline.

Speed, integration, and industry knowledge are the foundation. Everything else is built on top of those three things.

## What Makes Us Different

**7-day delivery**
The average web agency takes six to twelve weeks to launch a site. We launch in seven days. That's not a marketing claim — it's how the company is structured. The onboarding questionnaire, the build process, the revision cycle, and the launch checklist are all designed around that timeline. Clients notice it, and it changes how they think about working with us.

**Integrated system, not a vendor stack**
Most businesses piece together a website from one vendor, SEO from another, and some form of follow-up that either doesn't exist or runs through a personal inbox. When something doesn't perform, everyone points at each other. Obsidion owns the whole stack — site, SEO, remarketing, and app — which means there's no handoff problem, no integration gap, and no ambiguity about accountability.

**Vertical expertise**
We build for businesses in specific markets: cannabis, automotive, salons and med spas, food and hospitality, contractors. That means the websites are built around how customers in those industries make decisions. The SEO strategy reflects how those searches actually work. The compliance considerations are already factored in. A generic agency starts from zero every time — we come in with context.

**Built for performance, not just presence**
A website that exists is not the same as a website that converts. SEO that gets impressions is not the same as SEO that gets calls. Every part of the Obsidion system is measured against a business outcome — leads, calls, bookings, repeat visits — not just deliverables.

## How We're Positioned

Understanding who we're up against on a call helps frame the value clearly. Three categories come up most often.

### vs. Generic Marketing Agencies

Full-service agencies typically offer the same products — websites, SEO, social, ads. The differences that matter:

- **Timeline**: Agencies run 6–12 week timelines for a website build. We deliver in seven days.
- **Vertical knowledge**: A generalist agency adapts. We specialize. There's a difference in quality between someone who has built forty dispensary sites and someone building their first one.
- **Accountability**: At a larger agency, the website team, the SEO team, and the ads team are often separate — sometimes separate departments, sometimes separate vendors. When results are missing, accountability diffuses. At Obsidion, one team owns the outcome.
- **Cost-to-value**: Agencies price for overhead. We price for output. The comparison tends to favor us when the client has been through an agency relationship and knows what they actually got for the money.

The framing on a call:

> *"Most agencies will charge more, take longer, and hand your project between three different teams who don't talk to each other. We're one system. If something isn't performing, there's no one else to point at."*

### vs. DIY Tools (Wix, Squarespace, GoDaddy, etc.)

DIY tools are the low-cost option that ends up being the high-cost option when you factor in owner time and what the output actually does.

- **Templates vs. custom**: Every DIY site looks like a DIY site. Template designs don't differentiate, don't convert, and don't reflect a brand the way a purpose-built site does.
- **Integration**: DIY tools don't connect to a CRM, a booking system, or a remarketing platform without significant manual work. The system doesn't exist — the owner becomes the system.
- **Maintenance**: Someone has to keep it updated, fix what breaks, and make changes when the business evolves. That someone is usually the owner, or the person who built it originally — who may no longer be available.
- **Compounding gap**: While the business owner is maintaining a Wix site, their competitor is running an optimized site on a managed system. The gap compounds over time.

The framing on a call:

> *"DIY tools are built so anyone can launch something. We build something that actually performs. There's a difference between a site that exists and a site that converts — and most DIY sites are solving the first problem, not the second."*

### vs. Vertical-Specific Platforms

Industry platforms like Weedmaps, DealerSocket, Vagaro, or similar tools are built for one vertical and optimized for their own ecosystem — not the client's.

- **Owned vs. rented**: These platforms typically host the business's presence on their platform, not on the client's domain. The business builds an audience on someone else's asset. If the platform changes, raises prices, or goes away — so does the presence.
- **Marketplace exposure**: Many vertical platforms are effectively marketplaces. Being listed alongside competitors means the platform profits from comparison shopping at the client's expense.
- **Single-function limitation**: A dispensary menu platform does menus. It doesn't do the website, the SEO, the follow-up, or the app. The client still has to piece together everything else.
- **No infrastructure ownership**: The business gets features on a platform. Obsidion builds infrastructure the business owns.

The framing on a call:

> *"Those platforms are useful for what they do. But you're building your presence on their asset, not yours. When we're done, you own the site, the SEO foundation, the follow-up system. That's yours regardless of what any platform does."*

## Culture

Obsidion operates the way it tells clients to run their businesses: with speed, accountability, and no tolerance for vague outcomes. A few principles that describe how work actually happens here.

**Speed is a value, not just a feature.**
Seven-day delivery is how we're structured externally. Internally, it means decisions get made, things get shipped, and "let me circle back on that" is not an acceptable answer when something is ready to move. Urgency isn't manufactured — it's the default.

**Direct communication over comfortable communication.**
If something isn't working, that gets said clearly — to clients and internally. The tone across all Obsidion content is direct, assumes the audience is capable, and skips the filler. That's intentional, and it carries into how the team communicates. Feedback is specific, expectations are explicit, and clarity is more useful than niceness when the two conflict.

**Execution over talk.**
A plan that doesn't ship is not a plan. Work is measured by what gets delivered and what it produces, not by effort or intention. This applies to the build team, to sales, and to every other function.

**Ownership, not task completion.**
The difference between doing a job and owning an outcome is the question: *does it work?* Task completion ends when the deliverable is handed off. Ownership ends when the outcome is confirmed. Obsidion operates on ownership.

**Respect for the client's intelligence.**
Clients are business owners running operations in competitive markets. They don't need to be managed or hand-held — they need straight information and a system that works. The same principle applies inside the company: people here are treated as capable adults who can handle real information and act on it.
