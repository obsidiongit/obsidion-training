# Product Knowledge — Overview

This section covers Obsidion's product line from a fundamentals standpoint — what each product is, how it works, and why it matters. The vertical-specific selling angles live in the playbooks (e.g., how to pitch SEO to a cannabis dispensary vs. a salon). This section is about understanding the products themselves well enough to explain them clearly, connect them to a prospect's specific pain, and answer questions without reaching for a script.

---

## The Four Products

### [Custom Websites](websites.md)
The foundation of every client engagement. Custom-built sites optimized for speed, conversion, and the client's specific industry. Not templates. Average 7-day delivery from kickoff to launch.

### [SEO Services](seo.md)
Local SEO and Google Business Profile optimization. Makes the website findable for people who don't already know the business exists — category and map searches in the client's market.

### [Remarketing](remarketing.md)
Automated follow-up via email, SMS, and ad retargeting. Catches the leads the website generates that don't convert on the first touch and works them through a sequence until they respond or opt out.

### [Mobile App](mobile-app.md)
Branded iOS and Android app for businesses with repeat customers. Loyalty programs, push notifications, online ordering or booking. The client's brand on their customer's phone — not a third-party platform.

## How They Work Together

Each product is useful on its own. Together, they're a system.

**The website is the hub.** Everything else is built on top of it. A slow, outdated, or missing website undermines every other investment — SEO can't rank what isn't there; remarketing has nowhere to send traffic; the app has nothing to reinforce. The site comes first.

**SEO makes it findable.** Once the site is built, SEO is what brings in customers who don't already know the business exists. Map rankings, organic results, GBP visibility — all of it drives traffic to the hub. Without it, the site only gets traffic from people already looking for the business by name.

**Remarketing catches what falls through.** Most visitors don't convert on the first visit. Most leads who call or fill out a form don't get followed up fast enough. Remarketing automates the follow-up so those leads don't go cold — email sequences, SMS triggers, and ad retargeting that keep the business visible until the prospect is ready.

**The app locks in the relationship.** For businesses with repeat customers, the app turns one-time buyers into loyal regulars. Push notifications, loyalty rewards, and direct booking on a branded platform — owned by the client, not rented from a third party.

The pitch framing for the integrated stack:

> *"We build the site as the hub, make sure people can find it on Google, automate the follow-up so leads don't slip through, and give your best customers a direct line back to you. Each piece connects — that's why it works better as a system than as individual tools you're trying to get to talk to each other."*

## Before Obsidion — Two Common Starting Points

Most prospects come in from one of two places. Knowing which one you're talking to changes how you frame the value.

**The fragmented-vendor client**
They have a website from one agency, someone doing SEO on the side, no automated follow-up, and maybe an app they're not using. Three vendors, no integration, and nobody owns the outcome when something doesn't work. The business owner is the person trying to make it all connect — and it's not connecting.

The pitch: *"Right now you're managing vendors. We're one system, one team, one outcome. When something's not performing, there's no finger-pointing between the website person and the SEO person — it's all ours to fix."*

**The DIY-and-neglect client**
They built a Wix site themselves, claimed a GBP listing and never optimized it, follow up on leads manually when they remember, and have no app. No one vendor to blame — just no system at all. They know something is off but haven't had time to deal with it.

The pitch: *"You've got the pieces but nothing's connected and nothing's running on its own. We build the system so it works without you touching it every day."*

## What to Know Before a Product Conversation

A few principles that apply across all four products:

**Sell the outcome, not the feature.** A prospect doesn't care that the site has schema markup — they care that they show up higher on Google. Don't lead with technical detail; lead with what changes.

**Connect every product to a discovery finding.** If they didn't tell you they're losing leads after hours, don't pitch remarketing. If they said their site is embarrassing, lead with the website. The product that maps to their stated pain is the one that closes.

**The stack sells better than any single product.** Individual products are useful entry points. The integrated system is the value proposition. As early as makes sense in the conversation, move from "we can fix X" to "here's how the whole thing works together."
